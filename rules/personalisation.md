# Personalisation Rules

How we write personalised cold outreach. These principles apply to every copy variable and email template.

## Infer, never state

The reader should think "this person gets it" not "this person read my website."

Never tell someone what they do. Show them you understand what it costs them.

- Bad: "I can see you do brand strategy for tech startups"
- Good: "When you're deep in a client rebrand, finding the next project tends to fall off the list"

Both show you know what they do. The second shows you understand their world.

## Describe the consequence, not the activity

People don't care that you know what they do. They care that you understand what it feels like to do it.

- Activity: "delivering enterprise sales training while managing keynotes"
- Consequence: "when you're delivering back to back keynotes, the next quarter's pipeline rarely gets a look in"

The activity is what their website says. The consequence is what they think about at 11pm.

## One concept per variable

Every variable contains exactly one idea. If you need "and" to connect two things, pick the stronger one and drop the other.

- Bad: "b2b salespeople and saas founders"
- Good: "salespeople"

## Simplicity beats specificity

A slightly broad variable that reads naturally is better than a hyper-specific one that sounds forced.

- Bad: "mid-to-large companies needing communications talent"
- Good: "enterprises"

If it sounds like a database field, it's wrong. If it sounds like something you'd say in conversation, it's right.

## Shorter is always better

When a variable works at 3 words, try it at 2. When it works at 2, try it at 1. The best copy variables are the shortest ones that still sound natural.

- 3 words: "enterprise sales training"
- 2 words: "enterprise sales"
- 1 word would lose meaning here, so 2 is right

## The read-aloud test

Before approving any copy variable, read the full email sentence aloud with the variable inserted. If it jars, even slightly, rewrite it. If you wouldn't say it to someone at a pub, it doesn't go in an email.

## Never use

- Em dashes
- Forward slashes ("and" or "or" instead)
- Capital letters for acronyms in variables (write "b2b" not "B2B")
- Trailing punctuation on variables
- "I can see you're..." or "It looks like you..." (too literal)
- "Worth a chat?" or "Let's jump on a call" (too pushy)
- Any language that sounds like it came from a CRM or a LinkedIn scraper
